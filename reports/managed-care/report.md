# Executive Summary

### Medicaid Managed Care Keeps Paying for People Who Should Not Still Be Enrolled.
The single most important finding across the 23 reports and 113 findings in the evidence bundle is this: persistent eligibility and enrollment control failures (concurrent enrollments, manual bypasses, incomplete redeterminations, weak incarceration/out‑of‑state matching) repeatedly generated material improper premium and capitation payments and remain a top program integrity risk for Medicaid managed care. Audits documenting these failures include state-level reviews that identified multi‑million dollar improper premium payments and federal/state analyses that flagged inadequate cross‑state detection and processing backlogs (see Report 53; Report 54; Report 32; Report 95). 

Across findings that included explicit dollar extractions, the summed exposure was approximately $160.04 billion — but that aggregate is dominated by two very large federal improper‑payment estimates and program‑level cost figures that are context items rather than directly auditable questioned costs (see Report 27; Report 21). Excluding those national/program‑context outliers, the audits identify hundreds of millions in discrete improper premium/capitation and claim overpayments (for example, roughly $220.6 million of identified improper premiums and capitation adjustments in state audits cited below).

## Background

### What Managed Care Oversight Failures Actually Look Like in Practice.
Managed care oversight failures in this bundle manifest as operational gaps that permit improper payments (capitation or fee‑for‑service), distort rate setting, or prevent correct reconciliation of plan financial reports. Practically, these issues are visible as:

- Eligibility system exceptions or manual “bypass” workflows that keep enrollment active after a member moves out of state, becomes incarcerated, or otherwise becomes ineligible — leading to duplicate capitation payments (example finding: eligibility bypass and MEDES indicator control weaknesses) (Report 79; Report 95).
- Untimely or missing reconciliations and attestations (AUPs, FSRs, MLR line‑items) that allow misreported revenues or excluded special payments to go uncorrected before rates are set (Report 23; Report 114; Report 11).
- Encounter and claims data that are incomplete, miscoded, or not used in rate‑setting or oversight (failed compound drug encounters, missing denied claim detail, sparse CPSP billing codes) (Report 7; Report 23; Report 39).
- Contracting practices (PBM “effective rate” or spread‑like pricing) that obscure returned funds and the true price paid to pharmacies, producing inaccurate pharmacy expense reporting that affects capitation and rebate calculations (Report 7).
- Weak governance, ERM and supervisory review processes that limit an agency’s ability to prioritize audits, remediate problems, and enforce corrective actions (Report 56; Report 26).

### The Pattern Is Broad: Eligibility, Payments, Data Quality, Pharmacy, and Governance.
The evidence bundle contains 23 audit reports and 113 findings from state and federal oversight activity (with 117 linked recommendations). The reports are diverse: state program audits documenting duplicated capitation and improper premiums (for example, Report 53 and Report 54), state‑level program reviews focused on service access and data limitations (Report 39), and federal analyses of managed‑care payment risks and improper payment estimation (Report 27; Report 28). Findings are distributed across themes: eligibility/enrollment issues (≈28 findings), capitation/payment and reconciliation weaknesses (≈20 findings), claims/encounter data quality (≈18), governance/internal controls (≈18), parity and program‑specific oversight (MH/SUD parity — Report 43; CPSP/perinatal services — Report 39), and pharmacy/PBM issues (Report 7; Report 26). The corpus mixes direct questioned costs and broader program‑level cost context. Only a minority of findings included explicit extracted dollar amounts (14 of 113), limiting confident aggregation of total questioned costs; where dollar amounts are cited below they come from those extracted values in the bundle.

## Key Findings

### Eligibility and concurrent‑enrollment failures produced repeat improper premium/capitation payments.
Why it matters: Active enrollments for individuals who are ineligible, incarcerated, or residing in another State directly cause duplicate capitation payments and improper managed‑care premium outlays. Eligibility controls are the first line of defense; when they fail, downstream reconciliations and payment controls must detect and correct the overpayments.

Evidence and examples
- Improper premiums due to third‑party insurance/disenrollment delays were documented in two state audits: Report 53 identified approximately $11.8 million in improper managed care premium payments over Oct 2023–Mar 2024 caused by delayed disenrollments; the report recommended reviewing and recovering the $11.8 million and disenrolling affected members (Report 53). Report 54 found $8,335,321 in improper premiums for April–September 2024 tied to disenrollment process failures (Report 54).
- Concurrent enrollment across states and resulting duplicate premium payments were central findings in multi‑state/concurrent enrollment reviews: Report 32 estimated Washington paid an average $8.6 million annually for long‑term concurrent enrollees and estimated over $135 million nationwide in unneeded premiums during the last year of the public health emergency (Report 32). Report 95 documented California paid $36.4 million in capitation for August 2021 on behalf of enrollees concurrently enrolled in another state and estimated roughly $19.9 million in erroneous capitation payments for residents concurrently enrolled elsewhere (Report 95).
- Operational causes were explicit: manual MEDES bypasses that prevented system deactivations (Report 79), insufficient notifications and processing of PARIS matches (Report 95), and incomplete redetermination processes that left duplicate or misclassified enrollees in the system (Report 21; Report 23). Report 23 also documented duplicative monthly capitation payments totaling $590,237 and an inability to provide complete paid and denied claim detail to support monitoring (Report 23).

Why auditors should probe this area: these findings show both high monetary impact at scale (state‑level multi‑million exposures) and recurring operational points of failure that are auditable using system extracts (eligibility, PARIS/T‑MSIS, capitation payments).

### Missing AUPs, Incomplete FSRs, and MLR Errors Increased the Risk of Distorted Payments.
Why it matters: Incorrect or incomplete MCO financial statements and FSRs (and missing AUPs) impair a state’s ability to calculate accurate Medical Loss Ratios, make correct remittances, and set actuarially sound capitation rates. These are systemic integrity risks affecting broad expenditure streams.

Evidence and examples
- Report 23 flagged that the agency could not provide complete paid claims, denied claims, administrative costs, or MLR calculations since 2012 and identified duplicative capitation payments — undermining the integrity of historical rate‑setting inputs (Report 23). The report also noted informational totals of $7.11 billion in capitation payments and $7.61 billion in fee‑for‑service payments for FY16, indicating the program scale at risk (Report 23).
- Report 11 documented errors in reporting templates and inconsistencies in MLR reporting (misreported taxes, QIA expenses, risk corridor amounts) and required corrections to MLR remittance calculations across plans (Report 11).
- Report 114 found that agreed‑upon procedures (AUPs) and year‑end reconciliations for MCO financial statements were not completed in a timely manner (2018–2021 AUPs missing before rate‑setting), and that FSR data were not consistently communicated to actuarial staff — creating a timing and information gap that could produce incorrect rates (Report 114).
- Operational examples: Report 7 observed that pharmacy expense reporting excluded returned funds from a PBM aggregation process (an “effective rate” arrangement), introducing a $26.4 million discrepancy in reported pharmacy expenses used for capitation considerations (Report 7).

Why auditors should probe this area: asserting that FSR/AUP completion, MLR template accuracy, and inclusion of special payments are testable: select a sample of MCO FSRs, confirm AUP completion dates relative to rate‑setting deadlines, reconcile MLR numerator/denominator line items to underlying ledgers, and verify treatment of special payments (SDP/pass‑through) per Report 11 and Report 114.

### Incomplete and Miscoded Data Weakened Both Monitoring and Payment Accuracy.
Why it matters: Encounter and claims data are used for quality monitoring, utilization measurement, and rate setting. Missing encounters, miscoding, or failure to resubmit rejected encounters mean services were not accounted for accurately and can create overpayments or undercount service use.

Evidence and examples
- Report 7 documented that a Health Plan failed to report 1,297 encounters for compound drugs due to coding errors, totaling $132,511, and did not correct and resubmit those claims (Report 7).
- Report 23 found encounter data were incomplete and not used to set 2016 capitation rates, weakening rate accuracy and oversight (Report 23).
- State audit work in Report 53 and Report 54 produced multiple discrete claim overpayments tied to coding and reporting errors (Report 53 identified inpatient overpayments of $1,969,028 and pharmacy claims totaling $394,898; Report 54 reported inpatient/clinic overpayments of $222,220 and newborn/maternity capitation misreporting adjustments totaling $1,341,969) (Report 53; Report 54).
- Program‑specific example: CPSP/perinatal service utilization measurement was severely constrained by non‑exclusive billing codes and low coverage of reviews, limiting the ability to validate managed‑care reported utilization (Report 39).

Why auditors should probe this area: audit procedures should include end‑to‑end matching of paid claims to encounter extracts, targeted billing code reviews (e.g., compound drug line items, newborn/maternity coding), and testing of the process for identifying, correcting, and resubmitting rejected encounters (see Report 7; Report 54). Sampling edits should be stratified by high‑risk service lines (pharmacy, inpatient newborn, transportation).

### Pharmacy Costs Became Harder to Verify When PBM Arrangements Obscured the Money Flow.
Why it matters: PBM pricing methodologies that aggregate Medicaid and non‑Medicaid business (effective‑rate or spread‑like arrangements) can hide returned funds and distort the pharmacy expense inputs used for capitation and experience‑rebate calculations.

Evidence and examples
- Report 7 found the Health Plan’s fiscal year 2018 FSR under‑reported pharmacy expense because it excluded funds pharmacies were required to return to the PBM under an “effective rate” contract; the report identified $26.4 million in pharmacy expense reporting that did not reflect final amounts paid to pharmacies (Report 7). The audit concluded the PBM methodology reduced price transparency and complicated validation of pharmacy payments (Report 7).
- Oversight recommendations included auditing PBMs as part of performance audit plans and requiring MCOs to produce underlying vendor data to verify expense allocations (Report 26; Report 11).

Why auditors should probe this area: auditors should obtain PBM contracts, reconciliation schedules between claim‑level payments and PBM remittances, and test whether returned funds are recorded and flowed through to FSRs. Procedures should include contract‑term review for spread/aggregation clauses and reconciliation of paid pharmacy claims to amounts reported as pharmacy expense in FSRs (Report 7; Report 26).

### Weak Governance Helped Known Problems Stay Unfixed.
Why it matters: Even when audits identify problems, weak governance and control frameworks inhibit timely corrective action and enforcement. Without documented ERM, supervisory review policies, and a formal corrective‑action monitoring process, problems recur.

Evidence and examples
- Report 56 assessed enterprise governance and found missing internal control frameworks, incomplete documentation of monitoring and noncompliance resolution procedures, and high‑risk weaknesses in board oversight, succession planning, and reporting (Report 56).
- Report 26 and Report 112 documented weaknesses in implementing performance audits, issuing and verifying corrective action plans, and supervisory review deficiencies for compliance action forms and grievance data summaries (Report 26; Report 112).
- Federal oversight reports noted inconsistent enforcement in programs like the RAC and inconsistent communication of exemptions, reducing recovery opportunities (Report 17; Report 12).

Why auditors should probe this area: audit programs should examine whether corrective‑action plans are tracked centrally, whether implementation evidence is required and validated, and whether an ERM framework (Green Book/COSO) has been formally adopted and embedded into prioritization of program integrity resources (Report 56; Report 26).

## Cross‑Cutting Insights - Weak Data, Weak Controls, and Weak Follow-Up Form a Recurring Cycle

### Systemic versus isolated failures
- Eligibility/enrollment control failures and payment/reconciliation problems appear systemic across multiple states and at the federal oversight level. Multiple state reports (Report 53; Report 54; Report 95; Report 32; Report 23) document similar operational failures in disenrollment, PARIS/MATCH processing, and MEDES bypasses. This cross‑jurisdictional concentration suggests structural vulnerabilities in eligibility processing and federal/state data matching rather than isolated plan errors.
- Claims/encounter data quality and PBM transparency problems are concentrated in a subset of reports but have outsized operational consequences where they occur (Report 7; Report 23; Report 54).

### Recurring tensions and enabling relationships
- Poor data quality (encounters, denied claim detail) enables both improper payments and weak oversight: incomplete encounter files undermine MLR/FSR reconciliation and prevent detection of duplicate payments or underreported utilization (Report 23; Report 11).
- Weak governance and incomplete audit follow‑up amplify operational defects. For example, missing AUPs before rate‑setting (Report 114) interact with incomplete MLR reporting (Report 11) to produce systemic rate‑setting risk.
- Automated federal matches (PARIS/T‑MSIS) create detection opportunities, but staffing and process limitations (Report 95) and inconsistent federal guidance on RAC inclusion of managed care (Report 12; Report 17) limit their effectiveness.

### What is especially important or surprising
- The corpus shows that a relatively small set of operational control weaknesses (eligibility deactivation logic, PARIS/T‑MSIS match processing, PBM return accounting) repeatedly produce material dollar exposures when left uncorrected. The magnitude of federal improper‑payment estimates in the bundle (Report 27) underscores the potential scale when systemic issues are present, even if those national figures include multiple programs and are not direct single‑audit questioned costs.

## Financial Impact - The Quantified Exposure Is Large, Even Before Most Control Failures Are Fully Measured
Aggregate exposures and caveats
- Summing extracted dollar values included in the bundle yields an aggregate of approximately $160.04 billion across the findings that carried numeric extracted_amount values. That total is dominated by large federal and program‑level context figures (Report 27 — very large improper payment estimates; Report 21 and Report 23 — program cost aggregates) rather than discrete, auditable questioned claims. Report 27 alone contributes the largest shares of that sum (federal improper payment estimates cited in the corpus) (Report 27).
- Excluding the two large federal/program context outliers produces a more operationally actionable set of exposures. For example, identified state‑level improper premium/capitation amounts documented in the bundle (Reports 53, 54, 32, 95, and 23) sum to roughly $220.6 million in concrete, recoverable improper premiums and duplicative capitation payments identified by those audits (Report 53; Report 54; Report 32; Report 95; Report 23). Other claim‑level recoveries and adjustments (Report 53; Report 54) add tens of thousands to low millions of dollars in individual categories (inpatient overpayments, newborn/maternity claims, pharmacy claims).
- Important limitation: only a minority of findings in the bundle included explicit extracted dollar values (14 of 113), so the sums above underrepresent the total unpaid or recoverable dollars that could be found with systematic audit work. Many findings document control weaknesses that imply exposure without quantifying it.

## Recommended Procedures

### Start Where the Money Leaks First: Enrollment, Reconciliation, and Data Validation
- Audit procedure: Quarterly automated end‑to‑end reconciliation of enrollment feeds → capitation rosters → bank transfers. Use PARIS and T‑MSIS matches to identify potential concurrent enrollments; select a stratified sample of high‑risk matches (recent move flags, long‑term concurrent indicators) and perform 100% validation of residency within 30 days of match.
- Expected outcome: Rapid identification and disenrollment of ineligible concurrent enrollees and documented recoveries (see procedures recommended in Report 95 and Report 32).
(addresses Eligibility & Enrollment; Findings: Report 95; Report 32; Report 79; Report 53; Report 54)

### Require and test timeliness of AUPs/FSRs before rate‑setting; perform sample reconciliation of MLR line items to source ledgers 
- Audit procedure: For the most recent rate‑setting cycle, require evidence that all MCO AUPs and FSR reconciliations were completed and reviewed by actuarial before any rate was approved. Select a sample of MLR reports and reconcile SDP, pass‑through, and provider incentive line items to provider contracts and MCO ledgers (per Report 11 and Report 114).
- Expected outcome: Correction of template errors, inclusion/exclusion of special payments per guidance, and prevention of rate distortions.
(addresses Capitation/Payment Accuracy & Reconciliation; Findings: Report 114; Report 11; Report 23)

### Implement targeted encounter/claim validation audits for high‑risk service lines and resubmission processes 
- Audit procedure: (a) Extract rejected and missing encounter lists (by provider and service type). (b) For pharmacy compound drugs, newborn/maternity, transportation, and high‑cost inpatient claims, select judgmental samples and test coding, medical necessity documentation, and whether rejected encounters were corrected/resubmitted within policy timeframes. (c) For each failing case follow through to payment and recovery actions as per Report 7 and Report 54.
- Expected outcome: Quantified overpayments for recovery and process improvement on encounter resubmission.
(addresses Claims Processing & Encounter Data Quality; Findings: Report 7; Report 23; Report 53; Report 54)

### Audit PBM contracts and reconcile pharmacy claim payments to PBM remittances and FSR pharmacy expense lines 
- Audit procedure: Obtain PBM contracts and remittance schedules, test whether PBM returns/adjustments are correctly allocated to Medicaid lines of business, and confirm the final amounts retained by pharmacies are reported in MCO FSRs. Where contracts use “effective rate” or aggregation clauses, require reconciliations that separate Medicaid‑specific returns.
- Expected outcome: Recovery opportunities, improved transparency for pharmacy expense used in capitation and rebate calculations.
(addresses Pharmacy/PBM contracting; Findings: Report 7; Report 26)

### Strengthen governance testing: verify corrective‑action plan implementation and supervisory review controls 
- Audit procedure: Compile all open corrective‑action plans from prior audits; for a sample of high‑priority items (eligibility, payment reconciliation, PBM, MLR), verify evidence of remediation, dates of completion, and supervisory sign‑off. Test presence and use of a documented ERM framework and whether risk‑based audit selection is occurring (as recommended in Report 56 and Report 26).
- Expected outcome: Greater enforcement traction and reduction in recurrence rates.
(addresses Governance & Internal Controls; Findings: Report 56; Report 26; Report 112)

### Require MCOs to provide underlying vendor data and implement a data‑quality validation program for encounter/denial/appeal files 
- Audit procedure: Mandate contractual deliverables for raw encounter, paid/denied claim detail, and vendor subledger data. Implement routine EDV (encounter data validation) checks: member concordance, service date logic, place of service, and denial/appeal chronology with internal appeal evidence (per Report 9 and Report 39).
- Expected outcome: Better ability to measure utilization, enforce parity and program requirements, and detect improper payments.
(addresses Claims & Data Reporting; Findings: Report 11; Report 39; Report 26)

## The Hard Questions for Agencies: Can the Systems Actually Be Joined, Tracked, and Verified?

### Data availability and linkage
Can your eligibility, capitation, and paid claims extracts be joined deterministically (Member ID + service dates) and linked to PARIS/T‑MSIS/ incarceration matches within an automated pipeline? If not, what are the barriers to building that pipeline? (See Report 95; Report 79; Report 32.)

### Corrective‑action follow‑through
For prior audits with corrective actions (e.g., PBM audits, AUP timeliness, encounter data fixes), what percentage of high‑priority actions have verifiable implementation evidence? Which corrective actions reverted to noncompliance? (See Report 26; Report 56; Report 114.)

### PBM contract transparency
Do MCO contracts and PBM statements allow isolation of Medicaid‑specific returns and guarantee access to subledger detail? If not, what contract language changes are needed and can auditors obtain PBM remittance schedules for the last three fiscal years? (See Report 7; Report 26.)

### Recoveries versus prevention
Given staffing and resource constraints, what is the agency’s prioritized split between recovery‑oriented audits (finding and collecting overpayments) and systems/process audits (that prevent recurrence)? How would shifting resources toward automated match processing change recovery yield? (See Report 53; Report 95; Report 17.)

### Encounter data and rate setting
Are MCO encounter submissions currently used in any material way for prospective rate setting within your jurisdiction? If they are not, what governance or contractual changes would be required to make encounters a trusted rate input? (See Report 23; Report 11; Report 26.)
